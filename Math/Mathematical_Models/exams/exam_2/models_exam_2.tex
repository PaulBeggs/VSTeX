\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{9}
    \item (Murphyâ€™s Law, part III) A doctor of internal medicine with a busy practice expects to be called into the hospital to respond to a serious heart attack on average about once every two weeks. Assume that heart attacks in this physicianâ€™s patient population occur at random with this rate. One such emergency call is a challenge. Two such calls in a single day is a disaster.
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item How many heart attacks should the physician expect to respond to in a single year?
        \sol{
            If a heart attack happens once every two weeks and there are 52 weeks in a year, then the physician should expect 26 heart attacks per year.
        }
        
        \item Explain why the probability that \(n\) heart attacks during the year all occur on different days is
        \[
            \frac{365}{365} \cdot \frac{364}{365} \cdot \frac{363}{365} \cdots \frac{365 - n + 1}{365}.
        \]
        \sol{
            We start with the case of \(n = 1\). The probability that 1 heart attack occurs on any day in the year, is of course, \(365 / 365 = 1\). For \(n = 2\), we know there was a heart attack on a different day already, so there are now \(364\) out of 365 available days in the year, giving a probability of \(364/365\). Then, for the \(n\)th heart attack, it must avoid all other days occupied by the \(n - 1\) heart attacks. Thus, the available days left are \(365 - (n - 1)\), or \(365 - n + 1\) out of 365 days, giving a probability of \((365 - n + 1) / 365\). We find the probability of independent events happening in sequence by multiplying them together, giving the product of fractions above.  
        }
        
        \item What is the probability that the doctor has to respond to two or more heart attacks on the same day sometime this year?
        \sol{
            From part (a), we have that 26 heart attacks are expected this year. We want to know the probability of at least 1 heart attack appearing on the same day as another. This is the complement of the probability that all 26 heart attacks occur on different days, which we can compute from part (b). Thus, we find that the probability is 59.8\%.
        }

    \end{enumerate}
    
    


    \setcounter{enumi}{13}
    \item (Geometric distribution) In this problem, we investigate the discrete version of the exponential distribution. Suppose that arrivals occur at random times \(i = 1,2,3,\cdots\). Then, the time \(X\) between two successive arrivals has the geometric distribution
    \[
        P(X = i) = p(1 - p)^{i - 1},
    \]
    where \(p\) is the probability of an arrival occurring at time \(i\).
    \begin{enumerate}
        \item Show that \(P(X > i) = (1 - p)^{i}\). [Hint: Use the geometric series \(1 + x + x^{2} + x^{3} + \cdots = (1 - x)^{-1}\).]
        \sol{
            Let \(q = (1 - p)\) so that \(P(X = i) = p(1 - p)^{i - 1} = pq^{i - 1}\). The probability \(P(X > i)\) is the sum of probabilities that \(X\) takes any value from \(i + 1\) to \(\infty\). Thus, we have:
            \[
                P(X > i) = \sum_{k = i + 1}^{\infty} pq^{k - 1}.
            \]
            Since \(p\) does not depend on \(k\), we can move it outside the sum:
            \[
                P(X > i) = p \sum_{k = i + 1}^{\infty} q^{k - 1} = q^{(i + 1) - 1} + q^{(i + 2) - 1} + \cdots.
            \]
            Factoring out the \(q^{i}\) term, we get:
            \[
                P(X > i) = pq^{i} \sum_{k = i + 1}^{\infty} = 1 + q + q^{2} + \cdots.
            \]
            Using the hint, we are left with \(P(X > i) = pq^{i}(1 - q)^{-1}\). Substituting \((1 - p)\) back for \(q\), and we find:
            \[
                P(X > i) = pq^{i}(1 - q)^{-1} = p\frac{(1 - p)^{i}}{(1 - (1 - p))} = (1 - p)^{i}.
            \]
            
        }
\newpage

        \item Use part (a) to show that \(X\) has the memoryless property \(P(X > i + j \mid X > j) = P(X > i)\).
        \sol{
            Consider the following:
            \begin{align*}
                P(X > i + j \mid X > j) &= \frac{P(X > i + j \text{ and } X > i)}{P(X > i)} \\
                &= \frac{P(X > i + j)}{P(X > j)} \\
                &= \frac{(1 - p)^{i + j}}{(1 - p)^{j}} \\
                &= (1 - p)^{i} \\
                &= P(X > i).
            \end{align*}
        }
        \item Compute \(E[X] = 1/p\). [Hint: Differentiate the geometric series to obtain \(1 + 2x + 3x^{2} + \cdots = (1 - x)^{-2}\).] Explain why \(p\) is the arrival rate for the discrete process.
        \sol{
            Recall for a discrete distribution that 
            \[
                E[X] = \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} iP(X = i),
            \]
            so we get 
            \[
                E[X] = \sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} i pq^{i - 1}.
            \]
            Again, since \(p\) does not depend on \(i\), we can factor it out:
            \[
                E[X] = p\sum_{i = 1}^{\infty} iq^{i - 1} = 1 + 2q^{1} + 3q^{2} + \cdots.
            \]
            From the hint, we get that \(E[X] = p(1 - q)^{-2}\). Substituting \(q = (1 - p)\), we find:
            \[
                E[X]= \frac{p}{(1 - (1 - p))^{2}} = \frac{1}{p}.
            \]
            Thus, we expect it takes \(1/p\) trials to observe an arrival. To answer the last question, consider the case of us expecting 10 trials before an arrival. This implies \(10 = 1/p \implies p = 1/10\), which is exactly the rate we would expect before an arrival. Meaning \(p\) is the arrival rate.
        }
    \end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}
