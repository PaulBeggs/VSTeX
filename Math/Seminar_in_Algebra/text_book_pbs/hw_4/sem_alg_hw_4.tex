\section*{Cover Sheet}

For the first two computations, I just followed what we did in my notes. Exercise 4 is a little scuffed because I couldn't fit the ``\(+8\Z\)'' term, but at least I added a note. For Exercise 18, I wanted to define cases to give me resources to use in solving the proof. That is, by partitioning the kernel into two bins of either trivial or nontrivial, I can exploit properties of either bin. For the former, I was able to use Corollary 26.6, and quickly prove the one-to-one half. For the other half, I knew that I needed to map an element in \(F\) to 0, but first I needed to set up the mapping. So, I used the oldest trick in the book, and just multiplied by 1. Then, I could use substitution and associativity to get \(a\) alone. Then, after I applied \(\varphi\), I could use the fact that \(a \in N\) to get a zero to multiply by. This was what I needed to send \(x\) to 0. Exercise 22 involved a lot of drawing a lot of arrows on my scratch paper, and keeping track of where elements resided in the space. Doing part \textbf{a} first helped a lot, as part \textbf{c} was very similar, so I just had to modify it as needed. Exercise 24 was very similar to Exercise 18, so I was able to follow the same partitioning strategy that I did for that one. For the first part, I needed to show that the homomorphism was bijective. I got surjectivity because for every mapping \(x + \{0\}\), there is always an \(x \in F\). For injectivity, I just followed the definition, and the proof wrote itself. For the second half, I followed what I did for Exercise 18, but I needed to expand upon it to show that \(N\) was actually \(F\). This was done by just using the ideal property of \(a \cdot n \in N\) for \(a \in R\) and \(n \in N\). Then, for Exercise 26, I was very thankful that you had us do Exercise 50 all that time ago, so I could just plug that in. The second half of the proof was just computations. Exercise 29 was confusing to start off with. I didn't really understand what the question was asking until I played with the homomorphism mapping for a couple of elements. Then, I just applied the homomorphism property along with the definition of units. Finally, for Exercise 37, I just followed what I did for Exam 1 in the first semester of Algebra, except this time, I needed to show the additive group is also satisfied (we only showed that \(\gen{\C, \cdot}\) was isomorphic to \(M_{2}(\R)\)).

\section*{Section 26}

\textbf{Computations}

\begin{enumerate}
    \setcounter{enumi}{2}   
    \item Find all ideals \(N\) of \(\Z_{12}\). In each case, compute \(\Z_{12}/N\); that is, find a known ring to which the quotient ring is isomorphic.
    \sol{
        There are 6 ideals: \(\gen{0}, \gen{1}, \gen{2}, \gen{3}, \gen{4}, \text{ and } \gen{6}\). By computing \(a + N\) and \(a \cdot N\) for each ideal \(N\) and \(a \in \Z_{12}\), we find \(\Z_{12} / \gen{0} \cong \Z_{12}\), \(\Z_{12} / \gen{1} \cong \{0\}\), \(\Z_{12} / \gen{2} \cong \Z_{2}\), \(\Z_{12} / \gen{3} \cong \Z_{3}\), \(\Z_{12} / \gen{4} \cong \Z_{4}\), and \(\Z_{12} / \gen{6} \cong \Z_{6}\).
    }
    \item Give addition and multiplication tables for \(2\Z/8\Z\). Are \(2\Z/8\Z\) and \(\Z_{4}\) isomorphic rings?
    \sol{
        Distinct elements of \(2\Z/8\Z\) look like \(a + 8\Z\) for \(a \in \{0, 2, 4, 6\}\). In the following tables, we omit the \(+ 8\Z\) term for succinctness, but they should be in each cell. 
            \begin{center}
                \begin{tabular}[htbp]{c!{\vrule width 1.5pt}c|c|c|c}
                        \(+\) & 0 & 2 & 4 & 6 \\
                  \noalign{\hrule height 1.5pt}
                   0 & 0 & 2 & 4 & 6 \\
                  \hline
                   2 & 2 & 4 & 6 & 0 \\
                  \hline
                   4 & 4 & 6 & 0 & 2 \\
                  \hline
                   6 & 6 & 0 & 2 & 4
              \end{tabular}\hspace{1.25cm}
              \begin{tabular}[htbp]{c!{\vrule width 1.5pt}c|c|c|c}
                        \(\cdot\) & 0 & 2 & 4 & 6 \\
                  \noalign{\hrule height 1.5pt}
                   0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
                  \hline
                   2 & 0 & 4 & 0 & 4 \\
                  \hline
                   4 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\
                  \hline
                   6 & 0 & 4 & 0 & 4
              \end{tabular}
        \end{center}

    \item Let \(\varphi\) be a homomorphism of a ring \(R\) with unity onto a nonzero ring \(R'\). Let \(u\) be a unit in \(R\). Show that \(\varphi(u)\) is a unit in \(R'\).
    \pf{
        Let \(\varphi : R \to R'\) be a surjective ring homomorphism where \(R\) has unity \(1_R\) and \(R'\) is a nonzero ring. Because \(\varphi\) is surjective, the unity of \(R\) maps to the unity of \(R'\). Thus, \(\varphi(1_R) = 1_{R'}\). 

        Let \(u\) be a unit in \(R\). By definition, there exists an element \(u^{-1} \in R\) such that \(u u^{-1} = u^{-1} u = 1_R\). Applying \(\varphi\) to this equation, we get:
        \[
            \varphi(u u^{-1}) = \varphi(1_R) \quad \text{and} \quad \varphi(u^{-1} u) = \varphi(1_R).
        \]
        Then, using the homomorphism property of \(\varphi\), we can expand the left side of both equations:
        \[
            \varphi(u)\varphi(u^{-1}) = 1_{R'} \quad \text{and} \quad \varphi(u^{-1})\varphi(u) = 1_{R'}.
        \]
        This demonstrates that \(\varphi(u)\) has a multiplicative inverse, \(\varphi(u^{-1})\), in \(R'\). Therefore, \(\varphi(u)\) is a unit in \(R'\).
    }

    \item Show that \(\varphi : \C \to M_{2}(\R)\) given by
    \[
        \varphi(a + bi) = \begin{pmatrix}
            a & b \\
            -b & a
        \end{pmatrix}
    \]
    for \(a,b \in \R\) gives an isomorphism of \(\C\) with the subring \(\varphi[\C]\) of \(M_{2}(\R)\). 
    \pf{
        To show that \(\varphi\) is an isomorphism, we must prove it is a bijection and that it is a homomorphism. Notice that since the codomain of \(\varphi\) is defined as its image, \(\varphi[\C]\), the mapping is surjective by definition. To show that \(\varphi\) is injective, suppose \(\varphi(a + bi) = \varphi(c + di)\) for \(c,d \in \C\). Then, 
        \[
            \begin{pmatrix}
                a & b \\
                -b & a
            \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}
                c & d \\
                -d & c
            \end{pmatrix}.
        \]
        We see that \(a = c\) and \(b = d\), so \(\varphi\) is injective. Now, we show that \(\varphi\) is a homomorphism. First, we show that the additive property is maintained:
        \begin{align*}
            \varphi((a + bi) + (c + di)) &=  \varphi((a + c) + (b + d)i) \\
            &= \begin{bmatrix}
                a + c & b + d \\
                - b - d & a + c
            \end{bmatrix} \\
            &= \begin{bmatrix}
                a & b \\
                - b & a 
            \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix}
                c & d \\
                - d & c 
            \end{bmatrix}\\
            &= \varphi(a + bi) + \varphi(c + di).
        \end{align*}
        Now, for multiplication:
        \begin{align*}
            \varphi((a + bi)(c + di)) &= \varphi((ac - bd) + (ad + bc)i) \\
            &= \begin{bmatrix}
                ac - bd & ad + bc \\
                -ad - bc & ac - bd
            \end{bmatrix} \\
            &= \begin{bmatrix}
                a & b \\
                -b & a 
            \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}
                c & d \\
                -d & c
            \end{bmatrix} \\
            &= \varphi(a + bi)\varphi(c + di).
        \end{align*}
        Thus, we have shown that \(\varphi\) gives an isomorphism of \(\C\) with the subring \(\varphi[\C]\) of \(M_{2}(\R)\).
    }
