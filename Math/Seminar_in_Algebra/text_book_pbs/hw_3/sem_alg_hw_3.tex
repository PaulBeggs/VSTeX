\section*{Cover Sheet}

Exercises 1 through 8 were nice, as I just followed definitions. I will say that I really appreciate the evaluation homomorphism and its application. I think it allows us to essentially ``go back'' and use traditional substitution with the indeterminate values; only now, we have to leverage mapping properties to do it. For Exercise 22, I worked through this whole elaborate solution of showing that \(f(x)g(x) = 1\), only to find out that the same solution works by just setting \(f(x) = g(x)\), and the solution is much more straightforward (not as lengthy). So, I decided to rewrite it to cut down on the redundant logic. Exercise 24 was pretty difficult for me. Just like the previous problem, I started out with a premise that was overly convoluted and got stuck (I tried to use the definition of multiplication for \(D[x]\)). So, I started over, and went with a proof that followed from the definition of zero divisors: Take two nonzero elements, and show that their product is not 0. After that, I relied on the properties of integral domains, and that helped a lot. For the next Exercise, I had a lot of intuition following from the previous Exercise and Exercise 22. So, from them, I knew the points that I needed to hit with my description, and I just filled in the missing parts. Parts (b) and (c) followed directly from (a). \\

Then, for section 23, I had a blast with Exercise 2. Fancy \LaTeX\ table formatting (or \verb|array| formatting, in this case) is fun to mess around with. For the next Exercise, I followed the procedure outlined in the notes, and then I checked my answer with a Python script. Just for fun, I tried the next problem with the script, but it fell short because it couldn't find the \((2x + 3)\) root. Instead, it found 3 other roots, but they could not reassemble the original polynomial (because of the coefficient 2 that accompanied the \(x^{3}\) indeterminate), so I guess I need to work on it more. Exercises 10, 14, 16, 18, and 20 were all fun computation problems that just required using different tools that we established in the chapter. Exercise 31 was pretty hard. The hint was a godsend, as it really helped put things into a roadmap, but finding the total amount of reducible polynomials was the hardest part. However, breaking the task up into quadratics of the form \(x^{2} + ax + b\) helped, as I knew that I could factor those easier than the ones with a leading \(c\) coefficient (which I had to account for by just multiplying). Then, for the final proof, as soon as I saw the \(x^{p}\), I knew that we were going to do something with Fermat's Little Theorem. So, when I went back to Section 20, Corollary 20.2 was the key I was missing.

\section*{Section 22}

In Exercise 1, find the sum and the product of the given polynomials in the given polynomial ring.

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \(f(x) = 4x - 5, g(x) = 2x^{2} - 4x + 2 \text{ in } \Z_{8}[x]\).
          \sol{
            For addition, we can use the following formula to find the answer to \(f(x) + g(x)\):
            \[
                \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} (a_{i} + b_{i}) x^{i} = (-5 + 2)x^{0} + (4 - 4)x^{1} + (0 + 2)x^{2} = -3 + 2x^{2} \equiv 5 + 2x^{2} \text{ in }  \Z_{8}[x].
            \]
            For multiplication, we'll find the terms first, and then sum everything together afterwards. So, we start by finding \(c_{0}\):
            \[
                c_{0} = a_{0}b_{0} = (-5)(2) = -10.
            \]
            Then, for \(c_{1}\):
            \[
                c_{1} = a_{0}b_{1} + a_{1}b_{0} = (-5)(-4) + (4)(2) = 28.
            \]
            For \(c_{2}\):
            \[
                c_{2} = a_{0}b_{2} + a_{1}b_{1} + (0) = (-5)(2) + (4)(-4) = -26.
            \]
            Finally, for \(c_{3}\):
            \[
                c_{3} = (0) + a_{1}b_{2} + (0) + (0) = (4)(2) = 8.
            \]
            Plugging this into the multiplication formula to find \(f(x)g(x)\):
            \[
                \sum_{i = 0}^{\infty} c_{n}x^{n} = -10 + 28x -26x^{2} + 8x^{3} \equiv 6 + 4x + 6x^{2} \text{ in } \Z_{8}[x].
            \] 
          }
\end{enumerate}

In Exercise 7 and 8, \(F = E = \C\) in Theorem 22.4. Compute for the indicated evaluation homomorphism.

\begin{enumerate}

          }
    \item \(\varphi_{i}(2x^{3} - x^{2} + 3x + 2)\)

    \item  Find the number of irreducible quadratic polynomials in \(\Z_{p} [x]\), where \(p\) is a prime. [\textit{Hint}: Find the number of reducible polynomials of the form \(x^{2} + ax + b\), then the number of reducible quadratics, and subtract this from the total number of quadratics.]
          \sol{
            For the total number of quadratics (i.e., polynomials of the form \(cx^{2} + ax + b\)), we have to ensure that the leading coefficient is nonzero, so we have \(p - 1\) choices. For \(a\) and \(b\), they can be any element in \(\Z_{p}\). Thus, we have \(p^{2}\) choices, giving a total of \((p-1)p^{2}\). 

            From the hint, we should first consider the reducible polynomials \(x^{2} + ax + b\) first. Factoring these, we get polynomials of the form \((x - u)(x - v)\). We can account for all choices of distinct \(u\) and \(v\) by using the binomial coefficient \(\binom{p}{2}\), but we are missing the cases when \(u = v\), so we add in a \(p\) to account for every element in \(\Z_{p}\). Then, to get the total number of reducible polynomials, we multiply our total by the choices for the \(c\) coefficient (\(p -1\)). Thus, we are left with
            \begin{align*}
                (p - 1)\left(\binom{p}{2} + p\right) &= (p - 1)\left(\frac{p(p - 1)(p - 2)!}{2 \cdot 1 \cdot (p - 2)!} + p\right) \\
                &= (p - 1)\left(\frac{p(p - 1)}{2} + \frac{2p}{2}\right) \\
                &= (p - 1)\left(\frac{p(p + 1)}{2}\right)
            \end{align*}
            total reducible polynomials.

            Now, to find the total irreducible polynomials, we compute the following:
            \begin{align*}
                (p-1)p^{2} - (p - 1)\left(\frac{p(p + 1)}{2}\right) &= (p - 1)\left(p^{2} - \frac{p(p + 1)}{2}\right) \\
                &= \frac{1}{2}(p - 1)(2p^{2} - p^{2} + p) \\
                &= \frac{1}{2}p(p^{2} + p - p - 1) \\
                &= \frac{1}{2}p(p^{2} - 1).
            \end{align*}
          }
