\section{Introduction}

In abstract algebra, one of the main ways of identifying structure is through maps. We use maps such as homomorphisms and isomorphisms to build new sets with new structure. In other cases, such as factor rings of fields, there are only two options, as proven in \textit{this proof} wherein the factor ring of a field only has two choices: the trivial ring, or the field itself. Hence, we rely on maps to preserve, transform, and modify structure by shrinking or expanding groups, rings, and fields. 

Then, for maps, we also have the kernel of homomorphisms, in which \(\ker(\varphi) = \{r \in R \mid \varphi(r) = 0'\}\). I struggled with this concept quite a bit with groups, and had an even harder time when we revisited it with rings. Problem 6 details my problem with the kernel. That is, the definition of the kernel was difficult for me understand, as the definition did not tell me much at first. It wasn't until I understood that it was directly related to the mapping of elements. So, ``an element in the kernel'' directly translates to that element gets mapped to the 0 of the other ring in the map.
